Issues That Give People Influence

When people ask why I am running for Congress, I explain that this is a grassroots effort. I have never supported the traditional top-down approach to representation. Many residents rarely see their elected officials in the community. Decisions are made in Washington while the concerns of local neighborhoods receive limited attention. I believe representation should begin with regular engagement in the district and with a clear understanding of the daily challenges people face.

My approach focuses on what I call pocketbook items. These are structural issues that determine how much influence people have in public life. They shape the relationship between citizens and their government and they affect the ability of individuals to participate in the political process.

Influence belongs to the people, not political machines.
Trust grows when government actions are open and accountable.

Term Limits

Long political careers weaken the connection between elected officials and the communities they serve. Many people believe that regular turnover strengthens accountability. I support limiting House members to three terms and Senators to two. I also commit to serving no more than three terms in the House. This approach encourages new perspectives and keeps elected officials focused on the people who live in their districts.

Transparency and Accountability

Public trust depends on clear and accessible information. Many government records remain sealed for long periods of time, including files related to major historical events and investigations. I support releasing these records, strengthening protections for whistleblowers, and conducting regular audits. Transparency helps the public evaluate government actions and strengthens confidence in our institutions.

Clean Money in Politics

Campaign funding should reflect the interests of the people who live in our district. I support limiting donations to individuals and organizations within the jurisdiction. Large contributions from outside groups reduce the influence of local residents and shift attention away from community needs. Local voices should guide local representation.

Ballot Access and Independent Voters

In Pennsylvania, candidates who want to appear on the Primary Election ballot must collect signatures from registered voters during a three-week petition period that usually begins in mid-to-late February. This requirement applies to every candidate, but the impact is very different for those who are not running as Republicans or Democrats. Independent and unaffiliated candidates often need more signatures and do not have the organizational support that major party committees provide. These conditions make it extremely difficult for independent candidates to qualify for the ballot within the short timeframe.

This situation affects my own path as well. I consider myself a Constitutionalist and I do not align fully with either major party. To run a grassroots campaign that focuses on community involvement, I need a practical route that allows me to meet the legal requirements and reach voters. The Democratic field already includes several candidates, while the Republican field does not. This creates a clearer path for ballot access and community outreach. For this reason, I am running as a Republican in name while maintaining an independent approach in spirit and in practice.
